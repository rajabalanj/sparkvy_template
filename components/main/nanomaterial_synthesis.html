Nanomaterial Synthesis

1. Hydrothermal Synthesis

It is a method of crystallizing substances from high-temperature aqueous solutions at high vapour pressures, typically in sealed vessels called autoclaves. It mimics the natural geological processes that occur deep in the Earth’s crust. This technique is particularly useful for growing single crystals and preparing various inorganic compounds, including oxides, zeolites, and sulfides, under controlled conditions. The high pressure allows water to remain liquid at temperatures above its normal boiling point, facilitating the dissolution and recrystallization of materials. Read More

2. Solvothermal Synthesis

Solvothermal synthesis is a highly adaptable method used to create a wide range of advanced materials, especially at the nanoscale. It is similar to hydrothermal synthesis, but instead of water, it uses non-aqueous solvents under high temperature and pressure in a sealed vessel (typically an autoclave). This approach allows for greater control over the size, morphology, and crystallinity of the synthesized products, making it ideal for preparing metal-organic frameworks (MOFs), chalcogenides, and nitrides. 3. Sol-Gel Synthesis

Sol-gel synthesis is a versatile and widely used chemical method for producing solid materials—especially metal oxides—starting from small molecular precursors. The sol-gel process involves transitioning a system from a liquid “sol” (a colloidal suspension) into a solid “gel” phase. This gel is then dried and heat-treated to form the final material. This method is favored for its ability to produce highly homogeneous materials at low temperatures, with precise control over composition, purity, and microstructure, making it suitable for coatings, ceramics, and catalysts. 4. Green Synthesis

Green synthesis is an eco-friendly approach to producing materials—especially nanoparticles—using biological resources like plant extracts, bacteria, fungi, or algae instead of toxic chemicals or high-energy processes. Natural reducing agents (e.g., flavonoids, terpenoids, alkaloids) from plant extracts or microbes convert metal ions into nanoparticles. These same biomolecules often act as stabilizers or capping agents, controlling particle size and preventing aggregation. The process typically occurs at ambient temperature and pressure, making it energy-efficient and sustainable.
