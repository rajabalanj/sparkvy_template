1. Cyclic Voltammetry (CV)

Cyclic voltammetry (CV) is an electrochemical technique that measures the current response of an electrochemical cell as the potential of the working electrode is cycled linearly over time. This technique is widely used for studying the redox properties of electroactive species, determining reaction mechanisms, and characterizing electrochemical interfaces. It involves sweeping the electrode potential between two limits, causing oxidation and reduction reactions to occur, which are then observed as peaks in the current-potential plot. Read More

2. Linear Sweep Voltammetry

In analytical chemistry, linear sweep voltammetry is a method of voltammetry where the current at a working electrode is measured while the potential between the working electrode and a reference electrode is swept linearly in time. Unlike cyclic voltammetry, the potential sweep is unidirectional. This technique is often used for preliminary investigation of the electrochemical behavior of a system and can provide information about diffusion coefficients and reaction kinetics, particularly for irreversible processes. 3. Open Circuit Potential

Open circuit potential refers to the voltage that exists across the terminals of a circuit when it is not connected to any load, meaning there is no current flowing. This potential is crucial in various applications, particularly in electrochemistry, where it helps to determine the behavior of electrodes in an electrolyte. When electrodes are immersed in an electrolyte, they adopt an open circuit potential that indicates their ability to undergo oxidation or reduction reactions. Monitoring the OCP over time can provide insights into passivation, corrosion, or the stability of the electrode-electrolyte interface. Read More

4. Differential Pulse Voltammetry (DPV)

Differential pulse voltammetry (DPV) (also differential pulse polarography, DPP) is a voltammetry method used to make electrochemical measurements and a derivative of linear sweep voltammetry or staircase voltammetry, with a series of regular voltage pulses superimposed on the potential linear sweep or stairsteps. The current is measured immediately before each potential change, and the current difference is plotted as a function of potential. DPV is highly sensitive and is particularly useful for trace analysis of analytes due to its ability to discriminate against background currents. Read More

5. Chronoamperometry

Chronoamperometry is an analytical technique in electrochemistry where the electric potential of the working electrode is stepped, and the resulting current from faradaic processes is monitored as a function of time. This method is widely used due to its sensitivity and relative ease of implementation, although it is noted to be poorly selective. Chronoamperometry can be employed to interrogate reaction mechanisms and optimize electrochemical reactions. It is part of a family of step techniques, which also includes chronocoulometry and chronopotentiometry. It's particularly useful for determining diffusion coefficients and studying electrode kinetics. Read More

8. Galvanostatic Charge-Discharge (GCD)

Galvanostatic charge-discharge (GCD) is a fundamental electrochemical technique used to evaluate the charge storage performance of energy storage devices like batteries, supercapacitors, and pseudocapacitors. In GCD, a constant current is applied to the device, and its voltage response is measured over time during both charging and discharging cycles. This technique allows for the determination of key parameters such as specific capacity, coulombic efficiency, and cycle life. Read More
